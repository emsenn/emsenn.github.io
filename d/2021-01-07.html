Finding introductory reading on note-taking with Org-roam

Tomorrow, I'm going to help my partner as they begin to try and use Org-roam as their note-taking systems for their upcoming academic work; finishing their degree in library science.

So today, I'd like to share with them one or two pieces that I think might best prime them to engage with the activity. In this posting, I'm going to talk about my journey to find those resources.

To get started, I remember that when I was getting started with learning Org-roam, there were several lists to blog posts about the merits of a formal note-taking system, on the blog posts I was reading about Org-roam. I skipped past those lists at the time, as I was already sold on not just the intention but the specific product, but I'll try and go back now and re-find those things.

The place I started was with Jethro Kuan, and as my notes just reminded me as I typed in that link, I already wrote down the piece I wanted to remember, that talked about specifically this: "How To Take Smart Notes With Org-mode" Thank you, me of December 15th!

I'm going to switch over to my record on that piece, to cut out my favorite quotes, but I'll probably hop back and forth to here to talk about whether it's the right piece for my partner's self-education.

…And I'm back! The piece was a decent introduction to the flaws of the traditional academic research method, and introduced some benefits of accretive knowledge management, but wasn't as deep as I'd hoped. Maybe there's something to be found in Neil Mather's digital garden.

No such luck, but I did find an interview with Lion Kimbro that was useful. Lion Kimbro wrote "How to make a complete map of every thought you think".

The world of wikis that wasn't

I have found myself down an interesting rabbit-hole. I was, earlier, looking for readings to share with my partner about note-taking with Org-roam. I found an interview from 2004 with Lion Kimbro, about his book "How to make a complete map of every thought you think". The interview is on gilest.org, and here's my entry on that interview.

At the end of the interview, Giles Turnbull asks Kimbro to go back to an earlier tangent, about wiki and their role in what Kimbro calls the "Public Web," which seems contextually synonymous with my use of "the Web."

Again, this interview is from 2004. Here are some quotes, that I'll discuss. This is all in response to Turnbull asking Kimbro the question, "Tell me why you like wikis"

Wiki, briefly, are the hottest thing in Social Software right now. All this “friendster” stuff is important, but it’s just a ruse. People don’t see the real revolution that wiki represents.

Wiki is terribly important because it’s the first public communication system that is DOCUMENT based.

Instant Messaging, E-Mail, Internet Relay Chat (IRC), Online Message Boards, all this stuff-

It’s all MESSAGE based.

It’s a “quick signal.” You send a message to someone, and they do something different than they would have done before. “Someone.” Sometimes even a group of people. Maybe you’re on a mailing list, or something.

After the message is done, it’s spent. It’s indexed by Google, and someone looking for something can find your message amidst a sea of messages, and try to apply it to their situation.

But wiki is totally different. It’s document based. The fundamental element of a wiki is the document. It’s not a one-off message. (Though, you CAN attach messages to the bottom of a wiki page. Very common.) It’s something that people can use, and refer back to, time and time again.

It’s made to exist across time.

And so, also, that means that you get a sense of “space.”

I can't help but think about the recent pushes I've seen from people, toward "space"-oriented digital places, like the metaphor of a digital garden. It feels like over and over again, people try and do something with computers, and end up diverted for years before realizing it.

Let’s say we live in the year 2020. We haven’t killed ourselves off yet, as a species. You’re interested in writing for journalism. So you start off by searching the web for “writing.”

You find yourself on some wiki somewhere. By now, the public refinery system has implanted itself, and there’s wiki for every subject imaginable. You find yourself on some major “writing for science fiction” wiki. You go to a special page, that lists it’s relationships with neighbors. You find that there is a “general wiki for writing.” You go there, and find that it’s a wiki made almost entirely of redirections. You find a link to a wiki about writing for journalism. You find yourself on the writing for journalism wiki. Now you’re happy to be here, but you’re just curious- what is topically “near” the journalism wiki? You see that there’s a link to the journalism law wiki, you see that there’s a link to a plain talk writing wiki, you see links to all sorts of topically related wiki.

There’s a wiki for everything imaginable. All the information is maintained by the editors. There’s a refinery process that takes poor quality writing, half baked ideas, integrates them into wiki that are taken better care of, and then they are taken into wiki that have even higher standards. It’s all networked, so you can trace a path from any one subject to any other subject.

Reading this hours after being made aware of the on-going edit war on Wikipedia right now was… uncomfortable. Our expectations for the capabilities of the Web and the Internet have shifted incredibly since their introduction, and I don't think for the better.

Really, wiki are going to change the world. It’s quite clear. The progression is already extraordinary.

Wiki is going to make our society more democratic, because we’re going to have incredible clarity about things, and because we’re going to have all this free information. It’s going to be refined. It’s going to be visual.

Is there any group out there that doesn't underestimate their risk for cooption?

And I'm uncomfortably aware that much of what Kembro is talking about doing with wiki, is what I'm talking about trying to accomplish for myself, with my records catalog. I think a crucial difference is that I prioritize consent over democracy, and so don't have the same emphasis as Kembro does on public assets.

In the interview, Kembro links to https://communitywiki.org. Here's a quote from the frontpage:

Wiki movement is close to being dead. We all know that. This text will probably get read by just one person other than me which proves my thesis (hello, Alex, I decided to write this message to everyone instead of a private message). I can only imagine how this wiki used to be years ago. Lively, pulsating. Something like that. I’ve never been there.

Reading that, I thought, huh, I know an Alex who is involved in wiki stuff…. Curious if this wiki was as dead as it's homepage proclaims, I went to its page listing recent changes… only to see activity from both Alex Schröder and Lion Kembro, today!

I don't have time to go further down this rabbit-hole, but I'm excited to have discovered this little loop between note-taking, wiki, Alex Schröder, Emacs and Org-mode and then me, taking notes.

    The warrant first went out on December 16th, after the two "failed to produce the child nor was any proof of life provided," and on the 23rd they were arrested as fugitives. Yesterday, they posted bond.

    Relatives of Mildred Old Crow informed the Bureau of Indian Affairs back in November that they'd not seen her since July 2018.

    That is, she was last seen alive when she was six. Two years later, two people who were supposed to be her guardians might spend six months in jail and pay a total of $5,000 because she has been missing, potentially that whole time.
