You should write

There's been a lot of posts about blogs that have come across my radar in the past couple days. I think around me it maybe started with Juha-Matti Santala saying "You should start a blog today". In it, Satala says why you should start a blog, but notably doesn't focus on direct monetization at all. Instead, Santala says:

  1. Blog for yourself
    • You can use your notes on past work to help yourself with current projects
    • It can be nice to go see what you wrote about through the past year
  2. Blog for other people
    • While Santala's blog doesn't directly earn money, it is a large repository of personal work, demonstrating knowledge and past labor, making it a part of their professional portfolio
    • When other people ask questions, Santala can refer them to the relevant blog post as a starting poing.
  3. Improve your writing skills
  4. Gather a following

There's more to the post than just that, but that's about the basics. This inspired James Van Dyne to write a post, "Start a blog", a few days later. It continues adding onto why you should start a blog:

  • Develop your ideas through writing
  • Be your own reference
  • Have an honest record of your past
  • Own your own data

Just now, Jeremy Friesen has shared another post, about why he blogs. I'll just include my notes from that below.

The Why of a Blog? [2021-01-12 Tue]

  • Note taken on [2021-01-12 Tue 13:29]
    In this post, Jeremy talks about why he runs his blog: "the meta-cognitive process of writing down why I write.

    He leads by saying that writing, as an action, starts as the development of an idea. This is something I feel similarly too - in fact I think Jeremy was a part of a conversation I had a couple weeks ago about my choice of lexicon around records, and where terms like "idea" and "concept" fit into that.

    Jeremy includes a quote from Leah Price: "What We Talk about When We Talk about Books" that I really like:

    I'm confident that the experience of immersion in a world made of words will survive if and only if readers continue to cave out places and times to have words with one another.

    This is a tangent I won't dive into right now, but it reminds me of why I'm such a big fan of MUDs and view them as such a powerful tool for a society that has something like the Internet.

    Staying on topic, Jeremy goes on to share some benefits of them writing a blog:

    • Making connections with other people
    • Raising his own profile, to allow for the growth of himself and his associates
    • Making a connection with his own descendents

I want to just add on my own little thing to what they're saying:

You should just write, blog or not. You can always copy-paste from your writing into whatever "blog" you use. That's kind of what I've been doing with my own records catalog, where you, if you're not me, are probably viewing this post. It's been really useful, for all the reasons people above have said, even though most of the "communicating" I do isn't directly from these records, but from copy-pasting them into conversations on the Fediverse or through email.
