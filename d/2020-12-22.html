[2020-12-22 Tue 10:21]

I know I've said this like, five days in a row, but I think I'm finally coming out of the weeds, with regards to transitioning back to the Emacs and Org ecosystem for my information management and Web performance. I've got Org-roam capture templates and Org-roam daily capture templates set up for easily creating both permanent and fleeting notes as records, and a way to (simply, and with a lot of lost context) publish those records as a Website.

Emacs is a text editor that allows for a lot of customization.

Org-mode is a text-formatting language and its interpretter software, a part of the Emacs text editor.

I've been heavily inspired by seeing Jeremy Friesen's own Website, takeonrules.com. While the tools he uses to build the site are not entirely the tools I plan to use, there's a lot of overlap, and the presentation of his site is very close to the presentation I'd like mine to have.

I've also been using Neil Mather's Website as a bit of a reference - and just now noticed, actually, that Neil and I follow each other in the Fediverse and have been talking a bit about me preparing my own Website! Small world.

Hopefully, all this means that I'm far enough along that I can start using my Website to broadcast postings again, like I was doing briefly with my Wordpress, starting today. As I go I'll develop the other records that'll be stored on the emsenn.net.

I haven't written too much about it, but I see this digital garden as having potential as a tool for facilitating cross-cultural communication, and perhaps even having a role in me live-streaming, especially when such streams inevitably turn to the topic of decolonization.

[2020-12-22 Tue 13:13]

I saw someone link to the Open Infrastructure Map today and I clicked through and realized: there's no electricity in Chapel Hill, according to this map! There's no listing of the Manning Drive Power Plant and so the town whole downtown of Chapel Hill seems to lack electricity!

It's "funny," how erased the existence of that power plant is. People in town don't know it's there, even though the coal train blocks traffic flowing through Carrboro every day or so, sometimes twice! And I say erased on purpose; the University is being sued about the power plant, and does everything they can to pretend it, and the environmental racism lawsuit against UNC-CH, doesn't exist.

[2020-12-22 Tue 19:43]

So I want to post this here because every time someone seems me do it, they go :O

In the Org-mode manual it's well documented that you can name source blocks with a line starting with #+name: above them: 

#+name: demonstration-code-block-1
#+begin_src elisp
(print "practice gratitude")
#+end_src

(see 15.2 Structure of Code Blocks in the manual.)

You can also name tables, I found that documented in 15.4 Environment of a Code Block, and lists.

What I didn't find documented anyplace is that you can name quote blocks, and also just ordinary paragraphs.

I named that paragraph alpha-paragraph, now I'll work with it in Elisp: 

(print alpha)

That, behind the scenes, looks like this: 

#+name: working-with-alpha 
#+begin_src elisp :var alpha=alpha-paragraph :results raw :wrap quote
  (print alpha)
#+end_src

Evaluating that results in the following output:

What I didn't find documented anyplace is that you can name quote blocks, and also just ordinary paragraphs.

Conveniently, this also means I can use Org-mode's inclusion system to bring in paragraphs from other documents. The definition of Groundhog Emacs should be pulled in below:

Groundhog Emacs is a tool for Groundhog people doing information management using a computer.

That appears to work, at least at the time of writing. You can also use Org-modes link-handling system to easily link to a specific paragraph this way. Hope y'all find this useful info!

As requested by Benjamin Slade, here's the Org-mode all together: 

#+name: alpha-paragraph
  What I didn't find documented anyplace is that you can name quote blocks, and also just /ordinary paragraphs/.

#+name: demonstration-code-block-1
#+begin_src elisp
(print "practice gratitude")
#+end_src

#+name: working-with-alpha 
#+begin_src elisp :var alpha=alpha-paragraph :results raw :wrap quote
  (print alpha)
#+end_src

Put this in an empty Org-mode buffer and press C-c C-c over the last block and you should get the following: 

#+RESULTS: working-with-alpha
#+begin_quote
  What I didn't find documented anyplace is that you can name quote blocks, and also just /ordinary paragraphs/.
#+end_quote
